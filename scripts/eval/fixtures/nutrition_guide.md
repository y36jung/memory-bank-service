# Comprehensive Nutrition Guide

## Introduction

Good nutrition is the foundation of physical health, athletic performance, and long-term well-being. Whether you are training for a marathon, managing a chronic condition, or simply trying to eat better on a busy schedule, understanding the science behind macronutrients and micronutrients gives you the tools to make informed decisions. This guide covers the essential building blocks of the human diet, from protein and carbohydrates to vitamins and minerals, and concludes with practical advice on food safety when eating and travelling internationally.

Nutrition science is not static. Guidelines evolve as researchers publish new findings about gut microbiome diversity, the role of phytonutrients, and individual genetic variation in nutrient metabolism. The recommendations below reflect current consensus from major health authorities including the World Health Organization (WHO), the Dietary Reference Intakes (DRI) published by the National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine, and Health Canada's Canadian Nutrient File.

---

## Macronutrients

Macronutrients are the three primary classes of energy-yielding compounds in food: protein, carbohydrates, and fats. Every calorie you consume comes from one or more of these sources. A fourth caloric source, alcohol, provides 7 kcal per gram but is not a nutrient and will not be covered in detail here.

Understanding the role of each macronutrient helps you design a diet that supports your specific goals, whether those are body composition, endurance, cognitive clarity, or disease prevention.

---

### Protein

Protein is composed of amino acids linked by peptide bonds. Of the twenty standard amino acids, nine are considered essential because the human body cannot synthesize them in adequate quantities: histidine, isoleucine, leucine, lysine, methionine, phenylalanine, threonine, tryptophan, and valine. These must be obtained through diet.

Protein serves four critical functions in the body. First, it provides structural integrity — muscles, tendons, connective tissue, hair, and nails are all largely made of proteins. Second, it enables biochemical reactions as enzymes. Third, it participates in immune defence (antibodies are proteins). Fourth, it transports molecules through the blood (haemoglobin is a protein that carries oxygen).

**Protein Requirements for Active Adults**

Protein needs vary considerably with activity level, body composition goals, and sport type. Research consistently shows that active individuals require significantly more dietary protein than general population averages to support muscle protein synthesis, repair exercise-induced muscle damage, and optimize recovery.

For active men engaged in regular resistance training or endurance sport, current evidence supports a daily intake of **1.6–2.2 g of protein per kilogram of body weight**. The lower end of this range (1.6 g/kg) is sufficient for moderate-volume training aimed at maintaining muscle mass, while the upper end (2.2 g/kg) is appropriate during phases of aggressive muscle hypertrophy or caloric deficit when muscle-sparing becomes a priority.

For active women, the recommended range is **1.4–2.0 g of protein per kilogram of body weight** per day. Women's lower absolute lean mass and differences in anabolic hormone profiles mean that the upper bound is somewhat lower than for men, though the principle of higher-end intake during hypocaloric phases applies equally.

Protein timing also matters. Distributing intake evenly across three to five meals per day — aiming for 20–40 g per meal — maximizes muscle protein synthesis compared with concentrating most protein in one sitting. Post-exercise consumption within two hours is particularly effective.

**Protein-Rich Foods and Their Practical Properties**

High-protein foods vary significantly in their amino acid profile, bioavailability, and practical suitability for different contexts including travel, storage, and preparation.

- **Chicken breast (cooked)**: approximately 31 g of protein per 100 g. Complete amino acid profile. Requires refrigeration; not suitable for extended shelf-stable storage.
- **Canned tuna**: approximately 25–30 g per 100 g drained. Shelf-stable, convenient, and cost-effective. High in omega-3 fatty acids.
- **Beef jerky**: approximately **33 g of protein per 100 g**. Shelf-stable for months at room temperature. Low in fat (typically 3–10 g per 100 g). Compact and lightweight. An excellent travel protein source.
- **Mixed nuts** (almonds, cashews, walnuts, peanuts): approximately **20 g of protein per 100 g** combined, though this varies by nut type. Nuts also provide healthy unsaturated fats, fibre, magnesium, and vitamin E. Shelf-stable and calorie-dense.
- **Protein bars** (commercially produced): typically 15–25 g of protein per bar. Most commercially produced protein bars are **shelf-stable** due to low water activity achieved through manufacturing, and typically have a shelf life of 6–18 months. They are highly convenient for travel, work, and sport.
- **Hard-boiled eggs**: approximately 13 g of protein per 100 g. Complete, highly bioavailable protein with an excellent leucine content for muscle synthesis. However, hard-boiled eggs are **not suitable for long-haul travel** because they require refrigeration — unrefrigerated hard-boiled eggs should not be consumed after two hours at room temperature due to risk of bacterial proliferation (particularly Salmonella). They are suitable for short commutes or day packs with ice, but not for air travel or multi-day travel without a cooler.
- **Greek yoghurt (non-fat)**: approximately 10 g per 100 g. Excellent for gut health due to live cultures. Also requires refrigeration and is not suitable for unrefrigerated travel.
- **Edamame (cooked, frozen)**: approximately 11 g per 100 g. Complete plant-based protein. Requires freezing or refrigeration.
- **Lentils (cooked)**: approximately 9 g per 100 g. Excellent plant protein combined with dietary fibre and iron. Shelf-stable when dried (essentially indefinite shelf life), requires cooking.
- **Cottage cheese**: approximately 11 g per 100 g. High in casein protein (slow-digesting), making it popular as a pre-sleep protein source. Requires refrigeration.
- **Hemp seeds**: approximately 32 g per 100 g. Complete amino acid profile. Shelf-stable. Can be added to smoothies, yoghurt, or oatmeal.

When selecting protein sources for travel, prioritize shelf-stable options — beef jerky, mixed nuts, canned fish, and commercially produced protein bars are the most practical choices.

### Carbohydrates

Carbohydrates are the body's preferred fuel source, particularly for high-intensity exercise and brain function. They are classified as simple (monosaccharides and disaccharides) or complex (polysaccharides and fibres).

The brain alone consumes approximately 120 g of glucose per day at rest. During endurance exercise, muscle glycogen depletion is one of the primary causes of fatigue ("bonking"). Adequate carbohydrate intake prevents this.

**Dietary fibre**, a subtype of carbohydrate that humans cannot digest, supports gut microbiome diversity, reduces LDL cholesterol, moderates blood glucose spikes, and lowers colorectal cancer risk. Current guidelines recommend 25–38 g of fibre per day for adults.

The **glycaemic index (GI)** ranks carbohydrate-containing foods by how rapidly they raise blood glucose. Low-GI foods (oats, legumes, most vegetables) produce a slow, sustained glucose release. High-GI foods (white bread, sports drinks, white rice) produce rapid spikes — useful during exercise but potentially harmful when overconsumned at rest.

### Fats

Dietary fats are classified as saturated, monounsaturated, and polyunsaturated, with trans fats as an artificially produced variant now known to be harmful. Fats provide 9 kcal per gram — more than twice the caloric density of protein or carbohydrate — and are essential for fat-soluble vitamin absorption (A, D, E, K), hormone synthesis, and cell membrane integrity.

**Omega-3 fatty acids** (EPA and DHA found in fatty fish; ALA found in flaxseed, chia, and walnuts) have anti-inflammatory properties and are associated with reduced cardiovascular disease risk, improved cognitive function, and better pregnancy outcomes. Current recommendations suggest at least two servings of fatty fish per week or supplementation with 250–500 mg EPA+DHA daily.

**Trans fats** from partially hydrogenated oils raise LDL cholesterol and lower HDL cholesterol, significantly increasing cardiovascular risk. They should be eliminated from the diet entirely wherever possible. Many countries have now legislated their removal from the food supply.

## Micronutrients

Micronutrients include vitamins and minerals required in small quantities that are nonetheless essential for thousands of biochemical reactions. Deficiency — even subclinical deficiency — impairs immune function, cognitive performance, bone density, and energy metabolism.

---

### Vitamins

**Vitamin D** is synthesized by the skin upon exposure to UVB radiation and is found in few foods naturally. Deficiency is endemic in northern latitudes and among people who spend most time indoors. It regulates calcium and phosphorus absorption, supports immune function, and plays a role in muscle contraction. Recommended daily intake is 600 IU (15 mcg) for adults under 70, rising to 800 IU (20 mcg) for adults over 70. Many clinicians recommend higher intakes of 1,000–2,000 IU daily for those with limited sun exposure.

**Vitamin C** (ascorbic acid) is a water-soluble antioxidant essential for collagen synthesis, immune function, and non-haem iron absorption. The recommended daily intake for adult men is 90 mg and for adult women is 75 mg, with an additional 35 mg per day recommended for smokers. Rich sources include bell peppers, kiwi fruit, citrus, strawberries, and broccoli.

**Vitamin B12** (cobalamin) is found almost exclusively in animal products. It is essential for red blood cell formation, neurological function, and DNA synthesis. Vegans and people over 50 (who may have reduced intrinsic factor production and thus impaired absorption) should supplement. Recommended daily intake is 2.4 mcg for adults.

**Folate (Vitamin B9)** is critical for DNA synthesis and cell division, making it especially important during pregnancy — deficiency in the periconceptional period significantly increases neural tube defect risk. The recommended daily intake is 400 mcg DFE (dietary folate equivalents) for adults, rising to 600 mcg during pregnancy.

**Vitamin A** exists in two forms: preformed retinol (from animal sources) and provitamin A carotenoids (beta-carotene from plant sources). It is essential for vision, immune function, and cellular communication. The recommended daily intake is 900 mcg RAE (retinol activity equivalents) for adult men and 700 mcg RAE for adult women.

**Vitamin E** is a fat-soluble antioxidant that protects cell membranes from oxidative damage. The recommended daily intake is 15 mg (22.4 IU) for adults. Good sources include sunflower seeds, almonds, hazelnuts, and vegetable oils.

**Vitamin K** exists in two main forms: K1 (phylloquinone, from green leafy vegetables) and K2 (menaquinones, from fermented foods and animal products). Both are required for blood coagulation and bone metabolism. Recommended daily intake is 120 mcg for adult men and 90 mcg for adult women.

### Minerals

Minerals are inorganic elements essential to physiology. Unlike vitamins, minerals cannot be synthesized and must be consumed through diet or supplementation. Below is a comprehensive overview of the major and trace minerals required for adult health.

**Calcium**: The most abundant mineral in the human body by mass, calcium is essential for bone and teeth mineralization, muscle contraction (including cardiac muscle), nerve signal transmission, and blood clotting. Recommended Daily Intake: Adults 19–50: 1,000 mg/day. Adults 51+ (women): 1,200 mg/day. Adults 71+ (men): 1,200 mg/day. Tolerable Upper Limit: 2,500 mg/day (19–50 years); 2,000 mg/day (51+ years). Best dietary sources: dairy products, fortified plant milks, sardines with bones, bok choy, and tofu made with calcium sulphate.

**Magnesium**: Involved in over 300 enzymatic reactions, magnesium supports ATP production, protein synthesis, muscle and nerve function, blood glucose control, and blood pressure regulation. Recommended Daily Intake: Adult men: 400–420 mg/day. Adult women: 310–320 mg/day. Tolerable Upper Limit: 350 mg/day from supplements (no upper limit for food sources). Rich sources include dark chocolate, pumpkin seeds, legumes, almonds, and whole grains. Deficiency is common in Western diets.

**Phosphorus**: Phosphorus forms the structural backbone of DNA and RNA, is integral to ATP (the body's primary energy currency), and combines with calcium in hydroxyapatite for bone mineralization. Recommended Daily Intake: Adults 19+: 700 mg/day. Tolerable Upper Limit: 4,000 mg/day (adults); 3,000 mg/day (adults 70+). Found abundantly in protein-rich foods including meat, fish, eggs, dairy, and legumes.

**Potassium**: An electrolyte critical for fluid balance, muscle contraction, heart rhythm maintenance, and nerve impulse transmission. Adequate Intake: Adult men 19+: 3,400 mg/day. Adult women 19+: 2,600 mg/day. No established Tolerable Upper Limit for food sources; caution with supplemental potassium in renal disease. High-potassium foods include bananas, avocados, sweet potatoes, white beans, and spinach.

**Sodium**: Essential for nerve transmission and fluid balance. The body requires only approximately 500 mg/day, but most people in developed nations consume far more. Current guidelines recommend less than 2,300 mg/day for adults; those with hypertension, diabetes, or chronic kidney disease are often advised to stay below 1,500 mg/day. Excessive sodium intake is associated with elevated blood pressure, cardiovascular disease, and stroke risk.

**Iron**: A central component of haemoglobin (oxygen transport) and myoglobin (oxygen storage in muscle), iron also features in cytochrome enzymes involved in cellular respiration. Recommended Daily Intake: Adult men 19–50: 8 mg/day. Adult women 19–50: 18 mg/day (higher due to menstrual losses). Adults 51+: 8 mg/day. Tolerable Upper Limit: 45 mg/day. Haem iron (from meat) is significantly better absorbed (15–35%) than non-haem iron (from plants, 2–20%). Consuming vitamin C alongside plant-based iron sources enhances non-haem absorption.

**Zinc**: Necessary for immune function, wound healing, DNA synthesis, taste and smell perception, and normal growth and development. Recommended Daily Intake: Adult men: 11 mg/day. Adult women: 8 mg/day. Tolerable Upper Limit: 40 mg/day. Good sources include oysters (by far the richest source), red meat, poultry, beans, nuts, and whole grains. Vegetarians and vegans are at increased risk of deficiency due to lower bioavailability from plant sources (phytates inhibit absorption).

**Selenium**: A trace element that functions as a component of selenoproteins — antioxidant enzymes (glutathione peroxidases) and thyroid hormone-metabolizing enzymes (iodothyronine deiodinases). Selenium plays a key role in protecting cells from oxidative stress and supporting thyroid hormone metabolism. Recommended Daily Intake: Adult men: 55 mcg/day. Adult women: 55 mcg/day. Tolerable Upper Limit: 400 mcg/day. Selenosis (toxicity from excess selenium) can cause hair loss, nail brittleness, gastrointestinal disturbance, and neurological symptoms. Brazil nuts are exceptional selenium sources — a single nut can provide 68–91 mcg. Other sources include yellowfin tuna, halibut, sardines, egg, and whole wheat bread.

**Copper**: Involved in iron metabolism, collagen cross-linking, antioxidant defence (superoxide dismutase), and neurological function. Recommended Daily Intake: Adults: 900 mcg/day. Tolerable Upper Limit: 10,000 mcg/day. Sources include liver, shellfish, dark chocolate, and sunflower seeds.

**Manganese**: Plays a role in bone formation, amino acid and glucose metabolism, and antioxidant defence (manganese superoxide dismutase). Adequate Intake: Adult men: 2.3 mg/day. Adult women: 1.8 mg/day. Tolerable Upper Limit: 11 mg/day. Found in whole grains, legumes, nuts, leafy vegetables, and tea.

**Iodine**: Critical for thyroid hormone synthesis; deficiency causes goitre and hypothyroidism, and during pregnancy, cretinism in offspring. Recommended Daily Intake: Adults: 150 mcg/day. Pregnancy: 220 mcg/day. Tolerable Upper Limit: 1,100 mcg/day. Iodized salt and seafood are the primary dietary sources; dairy is a secondary source due to iodine content in cattle feed.

**Fluoride**: Strengthens tooth enamel and bone matrix. Adequate Intake: Adult men: 4 mg/day. Adult women: 3 mg/day. Tolerable Upper Limit: 10 mg/day. Fluoridated water is the primary source in many countries; excessive intake causes dental or skeletal fluorosis.

**Chromium**: Enhances insulin signalling and may improve glucose tolerance. Adequate Intake: Adult men 19–50: 35 mcg/day. Adult women 19–50: 25 mcg/day. No established Tolerable Upper Limit from food. Found in broccoli, grape juice, whole grains, and meat.

**Molybdenum**: A cofactor for several enzymes involved in amino acid catabolism and sulphite oxidation. Recommended Daily Intake: Adults: 45 mcg/day. Tolerable Upper Limit: 2,000 mcg/day. Legumes, grains, and nuts are good sources. True deficiency is extremely rare.

## Hydration

Water is not technically a nutrient but is essential for survival and all metabolic processes. The body loses water continuously through urine, sweat, respiration, and faecal matter. Adequate fluid intake is defined by the National Academies as 3.7 litres (125 oz) total water per day for adult men and 2.7 litres (91 oz) for adult women, including water from all beverages and foods.

Dehydration as mild as 1–2% of body weight impairs cognitive performance, reaction time, and endurance capacity. Athletes training in heat may require significantly higher intakes — losses of 1–2 litres per hour of intense exercise are not uncommon.

Electrolyte replacement becomes important during prolonged sweat losses. Sodium, potassium, magnesium, and chloride lost in sweat must be replenished to maintain neuromuscular function and prevent hyponatraemia (dangerously low blood sodium), which can occur when large volumes of plain water are consumed without electrolytes during ultra-endurance events.

## Food Safety During Travel

Travelling internationally introduces unique nutrition and food safety considerations. Customs regulations vary by country, and understanding them prevents confiscation of food at the border. The risk of food-borne illness from unfamiliar food handling practices also warrants attention.

### General Principles

The most common travel-related food illnesses are caused by contaminated water, improperly handled produce, and undercooked proteins. "Boil it, cook it, peel it, or forget it" remains a useful heuristic in regions with unreliable water safety or sanitation infrastructure.

Pack shelf-stable, protein-dense snacks for long journeys to avoid relying on airport food or in-flight meals, which tend to be nutritionally poor, high in sodium, and low in fresh vegetables.

### Travelling to Japan: Customs and Food Import Rules

Japan enforces strict customs regulations on the import of food products. Travellers bringing food into Japan should be aware of the following:

**Prohibited and restricted items**: Raw meat and raw poultry products are prohibited from entry without a permit issued by the Japanese Ministry of Agriculture, Forestry and Fisheries (MAFF). Fresh fruits and vegetables are also subject to import restrictions and are frequently prohibited or require phytosanitary certification, depending on the country of origin. Soil or soil-contaminated items are strictly prohibited.

**Commercially sealed, shelf-stable foods**: Japan does not restrict the import of commercially sealed, shelf-stable food products for personal consumption. Travellers may bring reasonable quantities of items such as beef jerky (commercially packaged and sealed), mixed nuts (commercially sealed), and commercially produced protein bars without issue. These items pass Japanese customs without restriction because they are processed, shelf-stable, and pose no phytosanitary or biosecurity risk.

**Practical recommendations for Japan travel**: For long-haul travel to Japan, pack commercially sealed beef jerky, mixed nuts, and protein bars as your primary protein sources. These are ideal — shelf-stable, high in protein, compact, and compliant with Japanese customs regulations. Avoid packing hard-boiled eggs (not shelf-stable), fresh produce, or any raw meats, as these will either require refrigeration (making them unsafe on a long-haul flight) or will be confiscated at customs.

**Alcohol**: Travellers may bring up to three bottles (approximately 2.25 litres) of alcohol for personal use without incurring customs duty.

**Quantity limits**: Japan allows a duty-free allowance on food items up to ¥200,000 in value for personal consumption. Commercial quantities may require an import permit.

### Travelling to the United States

The US Department of Agriculture (USDA) Animal and Plant Health Inspection Service (APHIS) enforces strict rules on importing food. Fresh fruits and vegetables are generally prohibited. Most commercially processed, shelf-stable snacks (beef jerky, nuts, energy bars) are permitted. Declare all food items on your customs form — failure to declare can result in significant fines.

### Traveller's Diarrhoea Prevention

Traveller's diarrhoea affects 20–50% of international travellers, depending on destination. Prevention strategies include: drinking bottled or boiled water, avoiding ice made from tap water, eating fully cooked foods, avoiding raw salads in higher-risk regions, and washing hands frequently. Probiotics (Lactobacillus rhamnosus GG and Saccharomyces boulardii) have modest evidence supporting reduced incidence when taken prophylactically.

## Putting It All Together: A Practical Framework

A nutritionally complete diet is built on a foundation of whole foods: lean proteins, complex carbohydrates, colourful vegetables and fruits, healthy fats, and adequate hydration. No single "superfood" substitutes for dietary variety, and no supplement fully compensates for a poor diet.

For active individuals, protein intake is the macronutrient most likely to require deliberate attention, since most people underestimate how much they need to support training adaptations. Micronutrient needs are generally met by a varied whole-food diet, though vitamin D, iron (particularly in menstruating women), B12 (in vegans), and omega-3 fatty acids warrant monitoring.

Use food safety knowledge as a force multiplier for nutrition: a diet well-planned at home can be maintained during travel by selecting the right shelf-stable, customs-compliant snacks.
