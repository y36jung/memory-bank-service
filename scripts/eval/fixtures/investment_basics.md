# Investment Fundamentals

## Introduction

Investing is the act of committing capital with the expectation of generating a return over time. Unlike saving — which preserves the nominal value of money in low-risk instruments — investing accepts some level of risk in exchange for the opportunity to grow purchasing power meaningfully above inflation. Over long time horizons, the historical return differential between invested assets and cash savings is substantial: global equities have returned approximately 7–10% annually in real terms over long periods, while cash savings accounts typically lag inflation.

This guide introduces the foundational concepts every investor should understand before allocating capital: asset classes, diversification, risk and return, and dollar-cost averaging. Mastery of these concepts is not sufficient for sophisticated portfolio construction, but it is necessary — and it is far more important to the average investor's outcomes than knowledge of any single investment product or strategy.

## Asset Classes

An asset class is a group of investments that share similar characteristics, behave similarly in the marketplace, and are subject to the same laws and regulations. The three primary asset classes are equities, fixed income, and cash equivalents. Real assets (real estate, commodities, infrastructure) and alternative investments (private equity, hedge funds) constitute additional categories more relevant to institutional or high-net-worth investors.

### Equities (Stocks)

An equity represents an ownership stake in a company. When you purchase a share of stock, you become a partial owner of that business and are entitled to a proportional claim on its earnings and assets. Returns from equities come from two sources: capital appreciation (the share price rising) and dividends (periodic distributions of company earnings).

Equities carry the highest volatility of the major asset classes. A diversified equity portfolio can lose 30–50% of its value in a severe downturn (as occurred in 2008–2009 and briefly in March 2020) before recovering. Over long time horizons — typically defined as ten or more years — the recovery potential and compounding effect of equities make them the highest-returning major asset class. For investors with shorter horizons, the sequence risk of a severe drawdown near a planned liquidation date is a real concern.

**Equity subtypes**: Large-cap equities are shares in large, established companies with market capitalizations typically exceeding $10 billion. Small-cap equities are shares in smaller companies, which historically show higher returns with higher volatility. Growth equities are priced on earnings expectations rather than current earnings; they tend to perform well when interest rates are low. Value equities trade below their intrinsic value based on current fundamentals and tend to outperform over complete market cycles.

### Fixed Income (Bonds)

A bond is a debt instrument through which an issuer (government or corporation) borrows capital from investors. The investor lends the principal, receives periodic interest payments (coupon payments), and receives the principal back at maturity. Government bonds issued by creditworthy nations (Canada, the United States, Germany) are considered very low risk. Corporate bonds carry higher yields to compensate for higher default risk, with high-yield ("junk") bonds carrying significant credit risk.

Bond prices and interest rates move inversely. When interest rates rise, existing bond prices fall (because new bonds offer higher yields, making existing bonds less attractive). When interest rates fall, existing bond prices rise. This relationship is important during rising-rate environments, where even "safe" bond holdings can produce capital losses.

Bonds serve a stabilizing function in a portfolio. During equity market downturns, investors often move into government bonds (a "flight to safety"), causing bond prices to rise at the same time equities are falling. This negative correlation — which is not consistent but has been historically common — is the primary diversification benefit of including bonds in a portfolio.

### Cash Equivalents

Cash equivalents are short-duration, highly liquid instruments that preserve capital and provide a small return. Examples include money market funds, Treasury bills (T-bills), commercial paper, and Guaranteed Investment Certificates (GICs) with terms under 90 days. They are not investment instruments in the growth sense — their primary purpose is liquidity and capital preservation.

Cash equivalents are appropriate for: emergency fund reserves, short-term saving goals (within one to three years), and the "dry powder" portion of a portfolio awaiting deployment.

### Real Assets

Real estate investment trusts (REITs) provide exposure to income-producing real estate — commercial properties, apartments, shopping centres — without requiring direct ownership. REITs are mandated by law in most jurisdictions to distribute at least 90% of taxable income as dividends, making them attractive for income-seeking investors.

Commodities (gold, oil, agricultural products) tend to perform well during inflationary periods when both equities and bonds may struggle. Physical commodity exposure is cumbersome; most retail investors access commodities through ETFs or commodity-linked futures funds.

## Diversification

Diversification is the practice of spreading investments across multiple assets, asset classes, geographies, and sectors so that the poor performance of any single holding does not severely impair the overall portfolio. It is sometimes described as "the only free lunch in investing" — through diversification, you can reduce portfolio risk without necessarily reducing expected return.

**Correlation and diversification**: Diversification works because different assets do not all move in the same direction at the same time. When assets have low or negative correlation with each other, combining them reduces overall portfolio volatility. A portfolio of 20 uncorrelated assets carries significantly less risk than a portfolio of 20 highly correlated assets, even if the individual assets have identical risk profiles.

**Intra-asset class diversification**: Within equities, diversification across sectors (technology, healthcare, financials, consumer staples, energy) prevents industry-specific events from devastating the portfolio. Diversification across geographies (North America, Europe, Asia-Pacific, emerging markets) prevents country-specific events (regulatory changes, currency crises, geopolitical shocks) from doing the same.

**The limits of diversification**: Diversification eliminates unsystematic risk (risk specific to individual companies or industries) but cannot eliminate systematic risk (market-wide risk). When a global financial crisis occurs, correlations between asset classes typically increase as investors sell everything to raise cash — the historical diversification benefit between equities and bonds, for example, can break down precisely when it is most needed. This is one reason investors also hold cash and alternative assets.

**Index funds and built-in diversification**: A total market index fund holds thousands of individual securities and provides instant diversification across the entire investable market. For most retail investors, a portfolio of low-cost index funds covering domestic equities, international equities, and bonds provides superior diversification compared with individual stock picking at a fraction of the cost.

## Risk and Return

The relationship between risk and return is the central trade-off in investing: higher expected returns come with higher risk. Understanding this relationship prevents two common mistakes — taking too much risk (which can cause panic selling at market lows) and too little risk (which causes long-term purchasing power erosion).

**Types of investment risk**:

- **Market risk (systematic risk)**: The risk that the entire market declines. Cannot be diversified away. Measured by beta — a stock with beta > 1 moves more than the market; beta < 1 moves less.
- **Credit risk**: The risk that a bond issuer defaults. Eliminated by holding government bonds of creditworthy nations; increased by holding corporate or high-yield bonds.
- **Liquidity risk**: The risk that you cannot sell an investment quickly at a fair price. High for real estate, private equity, and thin-market securities; low for publicly traded equities and government bonds.
- **Inflation risk**: The risk that returns do not keep pace with inflation, eroding purchasing power. Particularly relevant for cash and low-yield bonds held long-term.
- **Concentration risk**: The risk of overexposure to a single security, sector, or geography. Addressed by diversification.
- **Sequence-of-returns risk**: The risk that poor returns occur at a particularly damaging time — such as early in the drawdown phase of retirement. A 30% loss in year one of retirement has a far more damaging long-term impact than the same loss in year ten, because early losses reduce the base on which future compounding operates and may force liquidation of assets at depressed prices to fund living expenses.

**Standard deviation and volatility**: Risk is often quantified by the standard deviation of returns — how much an investment's return varies around its average. Higher standard deviation means more variable returns, which means wider potential outcomes in any given period. Canadian bonds have historically shown standard deviations around 4–6%; Canadian equities around 15–17%.

**Risk tolerance vs risk capacity**: Risk tolerance is psychological — how much volatility you can emotionally endure without making poor decisions (selling at lows). Risk capacity is structural — how much volatility your financial situation can absorb without real harm. Both matter when determining appropriate risk levels. A young investor with stable income and no near-term liquidity needs may have high risk capacity even if they have low risk tolerance.

## Dollar-Cost Averaging (DCA)

Dollar-cost averaging is an investment strategy where a fixed dollar amount is invested at regular intervals regardless of asset price. Rather than attempting to identify the optimal time to invest a lump sum — which is notoriously difficult even for professional investors — DCA deploys capital systematically over time.

**How DCA works mechanically**: Suppose an investor commits to investing $500 each month into a broad market index fund. In months when the market is down and the fund's unit price is lower, the $500 buys more units. In months when the market is up and the fund's unit price is higher, the $500 buys fewer units. Over time, this mechanical process results in a lower average cost per unit than would be achieved by investing fixed numbers of units each month.

**A numerical example**: Consider an investor using DCA over three months with the following prices per unit:

- Month 1: $50/unit → $500 buys 10.0 units
- Month 2: $40/unit → $500 buys 12.5 units
- Month 3: $60/unit → $500 buys 8.3 units

Total invested: $1,500. Total units acquired: 30.8 units. Average cost per unit: $48.70. The simple average of the three prices is $50.00 — the investor acquired units at a lower average cost than the average market price across the investment period. This is the mathematical benefit of DCA in volatile markets.

**Benefits of dollar-cost averaging**:

1. **Reduces timing risk**: By spreading purchases over time, DCA eliminates the risk of deploying all capital immediately before a market decline. No single investment decision can devastate the portfolio if contributions are spread across months or years.

2. **Enforces investment discipline**: DCA creates a mechanical, recurring commitment to invest. It removes the emotional calculus from each contribution decision. Rather than waiting for "the right moment" (which typically means waiting forever, since there is always a reason to hesitate), DCA deploys capital automatically.

3. **Effective in volatile markets**: DCA performs best when markets fluctuate significantly, because the variation in purchase price produces the greatest difference between average cost and average market price. In a highly volatile market, DCA's cost-averaging effect is most powerful.

4. **Accessible for regular investors**: DCA aligns naturally with how most people receive income — in regular paycheques. Investing a set amount each payday is a straightforward implementation that requires no special financial sophistication or access to large lump sums.

5. **Reduces emotional friction**: Investing is as much psychological as mathematical. Many investors struggle to commit capital during market downturns despite knowing intellectually that lower prices are opportunities. DCA sidesteps this by making the investment decision pre-committed and automatic.

**Limitations of dollar-cost averaging**:

1. **Lump-sum investing outperforms in steadily rising markets**: In a market that rises consistently without significant volatility, investing a lump sum immediately (LSI) outperforms DCA. This is because money invested earlier begins compounding sooner, and the averaging benefit of DCA is minimal when prices rise smoothly. Research from Vanguard (2012) found that lump-sum investing outperformed DCA roughly two-thirds of the time across historical market data for US, UK, and Australian markets.

2. **Transaction costs**: If an investment platform charges a fee per transaction, frequent DCA purchases generate more fees than a single lump-sum investment. This is largely mitigated by commission-free brokerage platforms, which are now the norm for retail investors, but remains a consideration for accounts with transaction fees.

3. **DCA does not eliminate risk**: DCA reduces timing risk but does not reduce the fundamental risk of the underlying investment. If the asset class purchased through DCA declines over the entire investment horizon, DCA will not prevent losses — it will spread them more evenly across time.

4. **Opportunity cost in high-confidence environments**: For investors who have a large lump sum available and high confidence in the long-term direction of the market, DCA means holding cash that earns less than the expected investment return for the duration of the DCA period. The risk-aversion rationale for DCA comes at a cost.

**DCA in practice**: For most investors receiving regular employment income, DCA is the natural and automatic investment strategy — each paycheque is a new contribution. The key practical decisions are the investment vehicle (index fund vs individual security), the allocation, and the contribution amount. Automating the contribution ensures DCA occurs consistently regardless of market sentiment.

## Putting It Together: A Simple Starting Framework

For first-time investors, a portfolio of three low-cost index funds often provides more than adequate diversification and a structure that is simple enough to maintain over decades:

1. **Canadian equity index fund**: provides exposure to the Canadian market, home-currency returns, and Canadian dividend income (which benefits from the dividend tax credit in non-registered accounts).
2. **International equity index fund**: provides exposure to global markets outside Canada, reducing home-country concentration.
3. **Canadian bond index fund**: provides income, capital stability, and partial hedge against equity downturns.

The allocation across these three reflects your risk tolerance and time horizon. A young investor with a 30-year horizon might hold 80–90% equities. An investor ten years from retirement might move toward 60% equities and 40% bonds.

Contribute regularly (DCA), rebalance annually, keep costs low (seek management expense ratios below 0.30% for index funds), and avoid reacting to short-term market movements. These practices, consistently applied over decades, produce the outcomes that sophisticated-sounding strategies rarely match.
