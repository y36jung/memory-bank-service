# Retirement Planning: Building Long-Term Financial Security

Retirement planning is the process of determining how much income you will need in retirement, identifying sources of that income, and building the savings required to fund the gap. Starting early is the single biggest variable within your control — the math of compound interest heavily rewards those who begin in their 20s and 30s.

## Why Starting Early Is Everything: Compound Interest

Compound interest is earning returns on your returns. Over long periods, it produces results that feel counterintuitive.

**The twin investor example:**

- Alex invests $5,000/year from age 25 to 35 (10 years, $50,000 total), then stops.
- Jordan invests $5,000/year from age 35 to 65 (30 years, $150,000 total).
- At age 65, assuming 7% annual returns: Alex has approximately $602,000. Jordan has approximately $472,000.

Alex invested 1/3 the amount and ended with more money, simply because compound growth had 10 more years to work. The lesson: **time in the market is the most powerful lever in retirement planning**.

## Account Types

### 401(k) — Employer-Sponsored Retirement Plan

A 401(k) is an employer-sponsored defined-contribution retirement plan. Contributions are made pre-tax (Traditional) or post-tax (Roth), reducing current or future tax burden respectively.

**2024 contribution limits:** $23,000 ($30,500 if age 50 or older — "catch-up contributions")

**Employer match:** Many employers match a percentage of your contributions (e.g., 100% of the first 3% of salary). This is free money. Never leave an unmatched employer contribution on the table — it is an immediate 50%–100% return on your investment.

**Investment options:** 401(k) plans offer a menu of mutual funds and target-date funds chosen by your employer. Options are often limited; choose the lowest-expense-ratio index funds available.

**Traditional vs. Roth 401(k):**

- Traditional: Contributions reduce taxable income now; withdrawals in retirement are taxed as ordinary income.
- Roth: Contributions are post-tax; withdrawals in retirement are completely tax-free.
- General rule: If you expect to be in a higher tax bracket in retirement than now, Roth is better. If you're in your peak earning years, Traditional may be better.

### Individual Retirement Account (IRA)

An IRA is a personal retirement account not tied to an employer. Two main types:

**Traditional IRA:**

- Contributions may be tax-deductible (depending on income and whether you have a workplace plan)
- Growth is tax-deferred
- Withdrawals taxed as ordinary income
- Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs) starting at age 73

**Roth IRA:**

- Contributions are post-tax (never deductible)
- Growth is completely tax-free
- Qualified withdrawals are tax-free
- No RMDs during the account owner's lifetime — accounts can compound indefinitely
- Contributions (not earnings) can be withdrawn any time without penalty

**2025 IRA contribution limit:** $7,500 ($8,600 if age 50+) — updated from the 2024 limit of $7,000 ($8,000 if age 50+).

**Roth IRA income limits (2024):** Phase-out begins at $146,000 (single) / $230,000 (married filing jointly). Above these thresholds, use a "backdoor Roth" — contribute to a Traditional IRA and convert.

**Optimal contribution order:**

1. 401(k) to capture full employer match
2. Max Roth IRA ($7,000)
3. Return to 401(k) up to annual limit
4. Taxable brokerage account for additional savings

### Health Savings Account (HSA): The Secret Retirement Account

An HSA is the most tax-advantaged account available. If you have a High-Deductible Health Plan (HDHP), you can contribute pre-tax, invest the balance in the market, and withdraw tax-free for qualified medical expenses. After age 65, withdrawals for any purpose are taxed like a Traditional IRA — but never penalized.

**The optimal HSA strategy:** Pay medical expenses out of pocket now. Save receipts. Let HSA funds compound in index funds. In retirement, reimburse yourself for decades of past expenses — tax-free.

## Target Retirement Numbers

**The 4% Rule (Withdrawal Rate):** A portfolio can sustain 4% annual withdrawals (inflation-adjusted) for 30+ years with very high probability. To find your target number: multiply your desired annual retirement income by 25.

- $40,000/year in retirement → need $1,000,000 saved
- $60,000/year → need $1,500,000
- $80,000/year → need $2,000,000

This is a simplified rule. Actual sustainability depends on asset allocation, sequence of returns risk, and retirement duration. With a 40+ year retirement (early retirement), a 3.5% withdrawal rate is more conservative and appropriate.

**Social Security:** Not a complete retirement solution, but a meaningful floor. Average benefit in 2024: approximately $1,907/month. Benefits increase by 8% for each year you delay claiming past full retirement age (up to age 70). If healthy, delaying to 70 is almost always mathematically optimal.

## The FIRE Movement

FIRE (Financial Independence, Retire Early) is a lifestyle and financial planning philosophy centered on extreme savings rates (40–70% of income) to achieve financial independence decades before traditional retirement age.

**FIRE variants:**

- **LeanFIRE:** Retire on $25,000–$40,000/year. Requires significant frugality in retirement.
- **FatFIRE:** Retire on $80,000–$100,000+/year. Requires larger portfolios ($2M+).
- **BaristaFIRE:** Semi-retire. Work part-time (e.g., at a coffee shop) for health insurance and supplemental income while the portfolio covers most expenses.
- **CoastFIRE:** Save aggressively early until your portfolio is large enough to coast to traditional retirement age without additional contributions. Stop saving, work any job you want.

The key insight of FIRE is that traditional retirement planning — save 10–15% of income for 40 years — is not the only option. Dramatically increasing savings rates compresses the timeline dramatically.

## Asset Allocation and Glide Paths

**Asset allocation** is how you divide your portfolio between asset classes (stocks vs. bonds). In general:

- Younger investors: 80–100% stocks (long time horizon absorbs volatility)
- Approaching retirement: Shift toward bonds to reduce sequence-of-returns risk
- In retirement: Roughly 60% stocks / 40% bonds is a common starting point, adjusted by spending needs and longevity

**Target-date funds** implement a glide path automatically. A 2055 fund starts aggressively growth-oriented and gradually becomes conservative as 2055 approaches. These are excellent set-and-forget options for 401(k) accounts.

**Sequence of returns risk:** The biggest retirement-specific financial risk. Poor returns in the first 5–10 years of retirement, while withdrawing from the portfolio, can permanently impair a retirement account even if long-term average returns recover. Mitigation: maintain 2–3 years of living expenses in cash/bonds as a buffer; avoid selling equities in down years.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Cashing out a 401(k) when changing jobs.** You pay income tax plus a 10% penalty and lose future compound growth. Always roll to an IRA or new employer's plan.
2. **Not increasing contributions with raises.** Lifestyle inflation is retirement's enemy. Bank at least half of every raise.
3. **Holding too much company stock.** Your income and investments should not both be exposed to the same company's fate.
4. **Underestimating healthcare costs.** A couple retiring at 65 is estimated to need $315,000 for healthcare costs in retirement, not counting long-term care.
5. **Claiming Social Security too early.** Claiming at 62 vs. 70 can mean a 76% difference in monthly benefit.
