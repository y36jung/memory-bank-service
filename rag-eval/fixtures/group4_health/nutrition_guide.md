# Nutrition Fundamentals: Fueling Your Body for Performance and Health

Nutrition is the foundation of health, athletic performance, and body composition. Despite the noise generated by diet trends, food industry marketing, and social media, the core principles of evidence-based nutrition are relatively settled. This guide covers macronutrients, micronutrients, caloric balance, and practical eating strategies backed by nutritional science.

## Macronutrients

Macronutrients are the three major categories of nutrients that provide energy. Understanding their distinct roles helps you make food choices that serve your specific goals.

### Protein (4 calories per gram)

Protein is composed of amino acids, the building blocks of muscle tissue, enzymes, hormones, antibodies, and virtually every structural component of the body. Of the 20 amino acids, 9 are "essential" — they cannot be synthesized by the body and must come from food.

**How much protein?** Current research strongly supports 0.7–1.0 grams of protein per pound of body weight daily for people who exercise. Sedentary individuals can maintain lean mass on 0.36 g/lb (the RDA), but this is well below optimal for body composition or performance. Higher intakes (up to 1.2 g/lb) show benefits for advanced athletes and during caloric restriction, when muscle is at risk of being broken down for energy.

**Complete vs. incomplete proteins:** Animal sources (meat, fish, dairy, eggs) contain all essential amino acids in approximately the right ratios — they are complete proteins. Most plant proteins are deficient in one or more essential amino acids. Eating a variety of plant proteins throughout the day effectively provides all essential amino acids. Leucine, in particular, is the primary trigger for muscle protein synthesis — it is abundant in whey protein, eggs, and meat, and lower in most plant sources.

**Protein timing:** Distribute protein intake across 3–5 meals of 25–40g each to maximize muscle protein synthesis throughout the day. A protein-rich meal within 2 hours post-exercise (not a strict window) supports recovery.

### Carbohydrates (4 calories per gram)

Carbohydrates are the body's preferred fuel for high-intensity exercise and the brain's primary energy source. They are stored as glycogen in the liver (approximately 100g) and muscle (approximately 400–500g). Glycogen depletion during intense exercise causes fatigue and declining performance.

**Simple vs. complex carbohydrates:**

- **Simple carbs** (sugars): Digest rapidly, spike blood glucose and insulin quickly. Useful for fast energy replenishment immediately post-exercise. Found in fruit, dairy, and added sugars.
- **Complex carbs** (starches, fiber): Digest slowly, producing a more gradual blood glucose response. Provide sustained energy. Found in whole grains, legumes, vegetables, and tubers.

**Glycemic index (GI) and glycemic load (GL):** GI measures how quickly a food raises blood glucose relative to pure glucose. GL adjusts for the amount of carbohydrate per typical serving — a more useful practical measure. High-GL foods chronically elevate insulin, which over time may impair insulin sensitivity. Prioritizing low-to-moderate GL carbohydrates (oats, legumes, sweet potato, brown rice, most whole fruits and vegetables) over high-GL processed foods (white bread, sugary beverages, most packaged snacks) is a foundational dietary principle.

**Dietary fiber:** Technically a carbohydrate but largely indigestible. Soluble fiber (oats, beans, psyllium) lowers LDL cholesterol and slows glucose absorption. Insoluble fiber (wheat bran, vegetables) promotes gut motility and reduces colorectal cancer risk. Recommended intake: 25g/day (women) and 38g/day (men). Most adults consume half this amount.

### Fat (9 calories per gram)

Fat is the most energy-dense macronutrient and serves critical functions: hormone synthesis (including testosterone and estrogen), fat-soluble vitamin absorption (A, D, E, K), cell membrane structure, and insulation. Dietary fat does not inherently cause body fat gain — caloric surplus does.

**Fat types:**

- **Saturated fat:** Found in animal products and some tropical oils (coconut, palm). Raises both LDL ("bad") and HDL ("good") cholesterol. Moderate intake is supported by current evidence; excessive intake is associated with cardiovascular risk in population studies.
- **Monounsaturated fat (MUFA):** Found in olive oil, avocados, and nuts. Associated with improved heart health. Primary fat in the Mediterranean diet, which has the strongest evidence base of any dietary pattern for cardiovascular outcomes.
- **Polyunsaturated fat (PUFA):** Includes omega-3 and omega-6 fatty acids. Omega-3s (EPA and DHA from fatty fish; ALA from flaxseed and walnuts) reduce inflammation, support brain health, and lower triglycerides. Omega-6s (from vegetable oils) are also essential but most Westerners consume excessive amounts relative to omega-3s. Aim for a ratio closer to 4:1 (omega-6 to omega-3) rather than the typical 15–20:1.
- **Trans fat:** Industrially produced (partially hydrogenated oils). Raises LDL, lowers HDL, and dramatically increases cardiovascular risk. Banned in the US since 2018. Still found in trace amounts in some processed foods and in naturally occurring forms in ruminant animal fat (where evidence of harm is less clear).

## Caloric Balance and Body Composition

Body weight is ultimately determined by energy balance: calories consumed versus calories expended. This is thermodynamics — it cannot be overridden by any food type, macronutrient ratio, or eating pattern. The popular notion that "a calorie is not a calorie" refers to the differential satiety, hormonal, and microbiome effects of different foods — but does not escape the fundamental energy equation.

**Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE)** has four components:

- **Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR):** 60–75% of TDEE. Energy to maintain basic physiological functions at rest. Primarily driven by lean body mass.
- **Thermic Effect of Food (TEF):** 10% of TDEE. Energy to digest and metabolize food. Protein has the highest TEF (25–30%), fat the lowest (2–3%), carbs in between (6–8%).
- **Exercise Activity Thermogenesis (EAT):** Deliberate exercise. Variable.
- **Non-Exercise Activity Thermogenesis (NEAT):** All movement that is not deliberate exercise: fidgeting, posture, walking, gesturing. Highly variable between individuals and one of the primary reasons metabolic rates differ between people of similar size.

To lose weight: create a sustainable caloric deficit (300–500 calories/day produces 0.5–1 lb/week of loss). To gain muscle: eat in a modest surplus (200–300 calories/day) while training. Extreme deficits impair muscle retention; extreme surpluses primarily add fat.

## Micronutrients Worth Knowing

**Vitamin D:** Acts more like a hormone than a vitamin. Critical for calcium absorption, immune function, mood regulation, and testosterone production. Synthesized from sunlight. Deficiency is widespread in northern latitudes and office workers. Recommended supplementation: 1,000–4,000 IU daily, especially in winter.

**Magnesium:** Involved in over 300 enzymatic reactions. Supports sleep quality, muscle relaxation, blood pressure regulation, and insulin sensitivity. Commonly deficient due to soil depletion. Food sources: dark chocolate, nuts, seeds, leafy greens, legumes. Magnesium glycinate or malate supplements are well-tolerated forms.

**Iron:** Essential for hemoglobin and oxygen transport. Deficiency causes fatigue, impaired exercise performance, and anemia. Women of reproductive age and endurance athletes (especially runners, who destroy red blood cells through foot-strike hemolysis) are at highest risk. Heme iron (from meat) is absorbed at 15–35%; non-heme iron (from plants) at 2–20%. Vitamin C significantly enhances non-heme iron absorption.

**Omega-3 (EPA/DHA):** Anti-inflammatory, cardioprotective, and neuroprotective. Target 2–3 servings of fatty fish weekly (salmon, mackerel, sardines) or supplement with 1–2g EPA+DHA daily. Fish oil supplements are the most bioavailable form; algal oil is an effective plant-based alternative.

## Evidence-Based Dietary Patterns

Rather than following specific macronutrient prescriptions, adopting a well-studied dietary pattern provides a practical, sustainable framework.

**Mediterranean Diet:** Highest evidence base for cardiovascular health and longevity. Emphasizes: olive oil as primary fat, abundant vegetables and legumes, whole grains, moderate fish, moderate wine, limited red meat and processed foods.

**DASH Diet:** Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension. Proven to reduce blood pressure and cardiovascular risk. Emphasizes: low sodium, high potassium (fruits and vegetables), low saturated fat.

Both patterns share an emphasis on whole, minimally processed foods, abundant plants, healthy fats, and lean proteins — the common thread across virtually all evidence-based dietary recommendations.

## Hydration

Water is involved in every physiological process. Even mild dehydration (1–2% of body weight) impairs cognitive function, physical performance, and mood. The "8 glasses a day" rule is a convenient heuristic, not a precision prescription. A more reliable indicator: urine should be pale yellow. Dark yellow signals dehydration; completely clear may suggest overhydration (rare but possible).

Electrolytes — sodium, potassium, chloride, magnesium — are lost in sweat and must be replaced during and after prolonged or intense exercise. Sports drinks provide electrolytes but are calorie-dense; electrolyte tablets or powder without sugar are preferable for most activities under 60–90 minutes.
